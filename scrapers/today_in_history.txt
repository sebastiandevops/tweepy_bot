 2003, Iraq War begins On this day in 2003, U.S. President George W. Bush ordered air strikes on Baghdad, thus launching the Iraq War to oust dictator Saddam Hussein, who was believed (wrongly) to be manufacturing weapons of mass destruction.
 2014, American church leader Fred Phelps—who founded (1955) the Westboro Baptist Church, which became known for its strident opposition to homosexuality and the gay rights movement, as expressed on picket signs carried by church members at funerals and other events—died at age 84.
 2008, English writer Arthur C. Clarke—who was best known for his visionary science-fiction novels and for his work on Stanley Kubrick's hugely successful motion picture 2001: A Space Odyssey (1968)—died at age 90.
 1997, Dutch American artist Willem de Kooning, a major exponent of Abstract Expressionism and Action painting, whose series Woman I–VI caused a stir with its violent images and impulsive technique, died at the age of 92.
 1996, One of the worst fires in the history of the Philippines swept through a Manila discotheque, killing 159 of the 400 people in the nightclub, which was intended to hold no more than 35.
 1982, Argentine forces mobilized after a dispute between Argentine workers and British scientists on British-controlled South Georgia island, leading to Argentina's invasion of the Falklands two weeks later.
 1962, American musician Bob Dylan released his eponymous debut album to mixed reviews.
 1954, American journalist Jill Abramson, who was the first woman to serve as executive editor (2011–14) of The New York Times, was born.
 1931, Nevada legalized gambling, which paved the way for casinos in the state, most notably in Las Vegas.
 1920, Józef Piłsudski was named marshal of Poland.
 1918, U.S. President Woodrow Wilson signed the Standard Time Act, which established Daylight Saving Time in the United States; the legislation also gave the federal government oversight of the country's time zones.
 1911, The first International Women's Day was observed.
 1860, William Jennings Bryan, a Democratic and Populist leader and a magnetic orator who ran unsuccessfully three times for the U.S. presidency (1896, 1900, 1908), was born.
 1687, While on an expedition to find the mouth of the Mississippi River, French explorer René-Robert Cavelier, sieur de La Salle, was murdered by his men in what is now Texas; La Salle had previously claimed a large region around the river for Louis XIV of France, naming it Louisiana.
 1560, In the Conspiracy of Amboise, French Huguenot aristocrats failed to overthrow the Roman Catholic house of Guise.
 1452, Frederick III became the last Holy Roman emperor to be crowned by a pope, Nicholas V. 
 1853, Nanjing, China, is captured and renamed Tianjing (?Heavenly Capital?) by forces of Hong Xiuquan, leader of the Taiping Rebellion, one of the greatest civil wars in world history.
